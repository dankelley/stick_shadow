The image shows the shadow that would be made by a thin 2cm stick aligned perpendicular to a flat level surface at Halifax, Nova Scotia, Canada at the indicated time. The thick line emanating from the dot in the centre indicates the shadow of a stick placed at that dot. The dotted circles have radii 1cm, 2cm, etc.

The source code, stick_shadow.R, is written in the R language. It is run at regular intervals, to keep the webpage up-to-date. With minor alterations, it could be used for other locations and times.

Here's how to make a miniature solar observatory:

  • Print this on a letter-size sheet of paper.
  • Place the paper on a sheet of corrugated cardboard, foam, or a smooth carpet, that is in direct sunlight. The surface must be level.
  • Hold a toothpick or sewing pin above the dot at the centre of the diagram, and carefully press it straight down through the paper and the material below. This should be as vertical as possible, to get a proper shadow on the paper.
  • Carefully spin the paper around until the shadow made by the stick is aligned with the shadow drawn in the diagram.
  • Look at the "N", "E", "S" and "W" markings along the outer circle. These show the principle points of the compass. (This is the true compass, not a magnetic compass.)

Safety Note. Do not leave the toothpick or the pin in the sheet, especially if there are young children or animals in the household.

If you want to make a diagram for another location or time, download stick_shadow.R and modify lines 2 through 5 as appropriate, and then run this in Rstudio or another R program. You'll need to install the 'oce' package (and perhaps some other packages) first.
